01: Definition

What is a Problem?

It emerges when a person or multiple people share in a not-so-satisfying situation.

02: Prioritization

Is it worth solving?

Real-world complexity requires weighing multiple variables. If there's no friction, it's just a task.

SINGLE USER
Personal Inconvenience
→
MANY USERS
Worth Solving
Resource Scarcity

Do we have the time and talent? Some problems are currently "unsolvable".

Ethical Variables

Does the "fix" create new tension for other stakeholder groups?

Scalability

Can the solution grow, or is it a local "duct-tape" fix?

Teaching Tip: Shift the conversation from "What do you want to build?" to "Who are you building for and what constraints are holding them back?".
03: Empathy

The Diagnostic Tool

Empathy is about seeing the world at eye-level. It's the engine of human-centered design.

Case Study: The Pirate MRI

By empathizing with a child's fear, designer Doug Dietz transformed a clinical procedure into an adventure, dropping sedation rates by 70%.

05: System Mapping

System Mapping

Before defining a solution, we must see the whole system. Shallow problems have shallow solutions.

Visualizing the Ecosystem

Identifying stakeholders, touchpoints, and the hidden forces involved in the friction.

Teaching Tip: Use a simple Venn diagram or an actor-map to show how different people interact with the problem.
06: Framing

The Problem Statement

The POV Formula

[User] needs [Need] because [Insight].

The HMW Reframe

How might we change [System] so [User] finds [Value]?
