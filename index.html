What is Empathy?

Empathy is the ability to see the world through other people's eyes, to feel what they feel, and to understand their motivations and frustrations. It moves beyond observable actions to gain a deeper understanding of the mindset.

Understand

Gathering insights from real stories and experiences.

Observe

Watching behaviors without judgment or interference.

Feel

Connecting with the emotional core of the user's struggle.

Proto-Persona

Your First Guess ‚Äî Before the Research

What is a Proto-Persona?

A proto-persona is an assumption-based sketch of who your user might be. It's created before any research, using only your team's existing knowledge and gut instincts. The purpose is to align the team on a shared starting hypothesis, which you will then validate or correct through research.

Think of it as a "best guess" ‚Äî it's expected to be wrong in places. That's the point.

Proto-Persona vs Persona

Proto-Persona

  • Based on assumptions
  • Created in 15‚Äì30 minutes
  • Done before research
  • A starting point

Persona

  • Based on real data
  • Created after interviews & surveys
  • Done after research
  • A validated profile

ü™® Example: The Thirsty Crow

Name Kavi the Crow
Context A crow in a semi-arid landscape with scarce water sources
Assumed Behaviors Flies long distances to find water; tries multiple approaches; resourceful under pressure
Assumed Needs Reliable water access; sources with easy physical access
Assumed Pain Points Water exists but is unreachable; narrow openings; no tools to modify containers
Risky Assumptions We assume the crow persists instead of flying away. We assume pebbles are available nearby.

Surveys

Quantitative Empathy at Scale

What is a Survey?

A survey is a structured set of questions sent to a large number of people to collect quantitative data. It helps you understand the "What" and "How Many" ‚Äî turning individual stories into statistical patterns. Surveys are the breadth tool in your empathy toolkit.

When to Use

  • You need to validate assumptions from your proto-personas
  • You want to measure the scale of a problem
  • You need demographic or behavioral data to segment users
  • You're building personas and need quantitative backing

How to Design One

1
Define your objective. What exactly do you want to learn?
2
Keep it short. 5‚Äì10 min max. Every extra question reduces completion.
3
Mix question types. Multiple choice + Likert scales for data; 1‚Äì2 open-ended for depth.
4
Avoid leading questions. "Don't you think X is bad?" biases answers.
5
Pilot test. Send to 5 people first. Confused? Rewrite.

ü™® Example: The Thirsty Crow

Imagine surveying 100 crows about their water-access struggles:

Q1 How often do you struggle to access water? (Daily / Weekly / Rarely)
Q2 Most common obstacle? (Too deep / Too narrow / Not available)
Q3 Have you used tools (pebbles, sticks) to access water? (Yes / No)
Q4 Stress level when water is unreachable? (1‚Äì5 scale)
Q5 Describe a creative solution you found. (Open-ended)

üìä Finding: 78% of crows cite "too narrow" as the #1 obstacle. This validates and guides our design.

‚ö†Ô∏è Surveys tell you what people do, but rarely why. Pair them with qualitative tools (empathy maps, interviews) for the full picture.

Customer Journey Maps

Mapping the Full Experience Timeline

What is a Journey Map?

A journey map is a timeline visualization of a user's complete experience ‚Äî from first contact to outcome. It plots actions, thoughts, and emotions at each stage, revealing exactly where the experience breaks down.

Anatomy of a Journey Map

Stages
High-level phases (Discover ‚Üí Attempt ‚Üí Solve ‚Üí Outcome)
Actions
What the user does at each stage
Thoughts
Internal dialogue at each moment
Emotions
The emotional curve ‚Äî highs and lows
Pain Points
The gold. Where the curve dips ‚Äî your design opportunities

Persona

The Research-Validated User Profile

What is a Persona?

A persona is a fictional but research-based character that represents a segment of real users. Unlike proto-personas, every detail is backed by interviews, surveys, and observations. Alan Cooper introduced personas in 1999 as a way to make design decisions around real human needs.

What Goes Into a Persona?

1
Identity: Name, photo, archetype quote
2
Demographics: Age, occupation, environment
3
Goals & Motivations: What drives them
4
Frustrations & Pain Points: What blocks them
5
Behaviors: Observed patterns (from empathy maps)
6
Scenario: A "day in their life" narrative

ü™® Example: The Thirsty Crow

After surveys (78% cite "narrow openings") and empathy maps, we build a validated persona:

Name Kavi the Crow
Quote "The water is always there ‚Äî I just can't get to it."
Demographics Adult crow, semi-arid region, solitary forager
Goals Dependable water sources; minimize energy spent searching
Frustrations Narrow containers; no teammates; time pressure from heat
Key Behavior Uses tools (pebbles) creatively ‚Äî observed in 3/5 field studies
Scenario On a 40¬∞C day, Kavi spots a jug after flying 2 hours. Water is 15 cm below rim. He drops 27 pebbles over 10 min until water reaches his beak.

‚úÖ Every detail here comes from actual data, not assumptions ‚Äî that's the key difference from a proto-persona.

The Problem Statement

A good problem statement is solution-agnostic, measurable, and human-centered. It defines the problem clearly without prescribing how to solve it.

Formula

[User Persona] experiences [Pain Points] because [Root Causes/Insights].

How might we help them [Desired Outcome] so that [Measurable Success Criteria]?

ü™® Example: The Thirsty Crow

Kavi the Crow experiences frustration and exhaustion when trying to access water from narrow-necked containers because his beak cannot reach water below 15cm from the rim, and he lacks knowledge of which containers are accessible before investing flight energy.

How might we help him identify and access water sources efficiently so that he reduces wasted flight time by 60% and successfully drinks on the first attempt in 80% of cases?

‚úì Notice: This statement doesn't say "build a water fountain" or "provide a drinking straw" ‚Äî it leaves the solution space open while being crystal clear about success metrics.
