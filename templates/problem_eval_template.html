Problem Statement Quality Evaluator

Design Thinking — Define Phase — Academic Framework

📋 This evaluator synthesizes criteria from Design Thinking literature, Problem Framing research (Dorst, 2011), Reframing methodology (Wedell-Wedellsborg, 2017), and HCD standards (IDEO, 2015) into a structured diagnostic tool. Paste your problem statement below and evaluate it across 6 dimensions.
Your Problem Statement
Dimension 1 — Human-Centeredness

🧑 Does the statement center on a real human?

Source: IDEO Human-Centered Design Toolkit (2015); Stanford d.school "Define" phase

1.1 Named or specific protagonist? Does the statement identify a specific user, persona, or stakeholder — not a vague "people" or "customers"?
1.2 Grounded in empathy research? Are the needs and pain points derived from actual user interviews, observations, or ethnographic research — not assumptions?
1.3 Emotional context present? Does the statement acknowledge the user's frustration, difficulty, or emotional experience — not just a functional gap?
Dimension 2 — Solution-Agnosticism

🔓 Is the problem free from embedded solutions?

Source: Wedell-Wedellsborg, T. (2017). "Are You Solving the Right Problems?" Harvard Business Review

2.1 No technology or method prescribed? Does it avoid phrases like "by building an app", "using AI", "with a new system"? The problem space must remain open.
2.2 Passes the "elevator speed" reframe test? Wedell-Wedellsborg's classic check: Could the problem be reframed entirely? If the statement locks you into one solution direction, it's too narrow.
2.3 Free from the "Law of the Hammer" bias? Is the problem framed around the user's need, NOT around a tool or capability your team already possesses?
Dimension 3 — Frame Quality

🖼️ Is the problem properly framed?

Source: Dorst, K. (2011). "The Core of Design Thinking and its Application." Design Studies, 32(6)

3.1 Causal chain articulated? Does the statement explain WHY the problem exists — not just WHAT the problem is? (Dorst's "because [root cause]" principle)
3.2 Context and constraints visible? Does the statement situate the problem in a specific context (when, where, under what conditions), or is it context-free?
3.3 Avoids premature narrowing? According to Dorst's frame creation model, has the problem been examined from multiple angles before settling on this framing?
Dimension 4 — Measurability

📏 Can success be objectively measured?

Source: Creswell, J.W. (2014). Research Design; SMART criteria (Doran, 1981)

4.1 Quantitative success criteria? Does the statement include measurable targets (percentages, time reductions, frequency counts) — not just "improve" or "better"?
4.2 Observable and verifiable? Could a neutral observer verify through measurement or observation that the problem exists (and that a solution resolved it)?
4.3 Time-bound or scoped? Does the statement indicate a time frame, iteration cycle, or condition under which success should be achieved?
Dimension 5 — Scope Calibration

⚖️ Is the scope neither too broad nor too narrow?

Source: Brown, T. (2009). Change by Design; Cross, N. (2006). Designerly Ways of Knowing

5.1 Broad enough for multiple solutions? Could at least 3 fundamentally different solution approaches address this problem? If only one solution seems possible, the scope is too narrow.
5.2 Narrow enough to be actionable? Could a small team make meaningful progress on this within one design sprint (1-2 weeks)? "Solve world hunger" fails this test.
Dimension 6 — Ethical & Systemic Awareness

🌍 Does it consider broader implications?

Source: Buchanan, R. (1992). "Wicked Problems in Design Thinking." Design Issues; Papanek, V. (1971). Design for the Real World

6.1 Stakeholder awareness? Does the framing acknowledge that the problem may affect other stakeholders besides the primary user?
6.2 Free from harmful assumptions? Does the problem statement avoid reinforcing stereotypes, biases, or power imbalances? Could solving it inadvertently harm another group?
